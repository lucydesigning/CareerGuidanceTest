On refusing the either/or, carrying two versions of your profile, and why the conversations themselves are the work.

Hi. Your question resonated a lot with me. I'm a bit more senior than you in my career, and I recently went through a job transition where I was thinking about whether to be design only, or product only, or both. These were for more senior roles, Chief Product Officer, Chief Design Officer, mostly in consulting.

As someone who is now leading a design team in a consultancy, I can say that one of the best things you can do is to expand further into product. It doesn't mean you're leaving design behind. With GenAI and the shifts happening there, with so many people able to be a maker, I think you could be a really strong candidate as a product leader.

Sometimes we see product strategy and product management as one thing, and sometimes as two, and it depends on the role. But there are a lot of companies who would find your design background coming into product meaningful, who don't just want someone who has been a PM their whole life.

So what I'd suggest, and it's what I did in my last job search, and maybe you're doing it already, is to let yourself explore and try both. These things emerge after a lot of different conversations. Have a flavor of your profile and resume that is more design, and one that is more product. And maybe you can talk about product in terms of zero to one product strategy, which is where I think people who come from design play best, because you get to bring the user into the equation when you're thinking about what you should actually build. You bring that innovative, divergent thinking.

So maybe hold it a little less as either/or and more as and. Have your design flavor, have your product flavor, and keep exploring. Don't stop yet, and don't feel like you have to choose one or the other, because it's going to be the company, the role itself, the people you work with, what their aspiration is, and which ones can accommodate the constraints you have right now, that are going to matter.

My last reflection from my own job search is that even though the destination is getting a job, the journey is part of the destination too. You have to have enough conversations, enough interviews, meet enough people, to increase your chances but also to get more and more clear. You feel clear that design is where you'd be happiest, but if you look at the evolving landscape and find the right people to talk to, you may be really happy doing product strategy as well. Or you may decide it's definitely not what you want. Either way, if you don't go further, you won't feel good about the decision you make.

I hope I'm catching you at the right time in your search and that you haven't been at it forever already. I'd recommend not giving up, and pushing to the next level of your network, friends of friends of friends, to have more conversations and see where you can go. Don't give up. I know it's hard right now.
